# How to Build a Business Without Much Capital: 8 Lessons on the Road From 0 to €100K

*I gave this talk on stage at LOGIN 2025. This is the written version, with the lessons, the real examples, and the tools. Originally published at [arunasvismantas.eu](https://arunasvismantas.eu/izvalgos/kaip-sukurti-versla-be-kapitalo).*

The statistics are brutal: around **90% of businesses fail before ever reaching €100K in revenue**, and 20–30% close within their first year. The idea looks unstoppable, everything feels electric, and then reality starts landing punches.

I've spent more than 20 years building companies in Lithuania and abroad. Some worked, some didn't, but every one of them left a lesson. Here are the ones that matter most.

*(Video of the talk: https://youtu.be/dDxAc3GPziU)*

A quick word on where this comes from: I'm a serial founder, though I prefer to call myself a serial *tester*. I've raised more than €3M in venture capital for tech startups, built the Podbase and [iDenfy](https://www.idenfy.com) platforms and two digital marketing agencies, and today I'm building [Callsy](https://callsy.ai). Not everything succeeded. Here's what stuck.

## 1. You have either money or time

The first lesson is the simplest and the most ignored. When you build a business, you either have money or you have time. With money, you can hire people to build the product, go to market, run marketing and sales. With time, you'll have to learn and do all of it yourself.

The worst position is having neither. Ideally you have both, but a business can absolutely be built with only time, or only money. What matters is being honest with yourself about which one you actually have, and planning around it.

## 2. Sales come before product

The biggest mistake I see is the belief that you build something amazing and customers will come. That's product-led growth, and today it barely works unless you know your audience precisely and exactly how to reach them. It's far safer to start from sales: sell first, then build.

When I built Podbase with the founders of Burga, a print-on-demand platform for phone accessories, we had only a half-working MVP after three months. We went to market anyway and talked to creators. Our first client believed in us, we got about 1,000 orders in the first month, and everything broke: operations, manufacturing, invoicing. But that's when you learn the most important thing.

> In business, failure isn't the only problem. Success is a problem too. And it's usually success that stress-tests your system the hardest.

You can't ship something completely broken anymore, you'll lose trust. But if you iterate and fix relentlessly, it's worth launching as fast as possible. With Podbase we reached €100K in monthly recurring revenue within 18 months. You don't have to sell an idea, sell a working MVP, and something bigger can grow over the next 12–18 months.

## 3. Don't be afraid to ask

In 2018 we were building iDenfy, a remote identity verification (KYC) platform. We had no marketing knowledge, no go-to-market strategy, and little sales experience. But we had time and ambition. So I simply wrote to global tech media: Forrester, Gartner, Markets and Markets.

Markets and Markets replied that they were preparing a report and included us among the top 10 creators of that technology worldwide, next to multi-billion-dollar companies. Overnight we got around 50 inbound leads. Another example, with Callsy: we had a legal question and I didn't want to spend money on lawyers, so I just emailed Sorainen. A lawyer replied and explained everything for free.

The lesson is clear: if you don't have money, you can always ask. People help. They really do. And luck tends to follow the people who aren't afraid to ask for it.

## 4. Pricing: the 3x rule and margins

I talk to about 50 business owners a month, and most think surprisingly little about pricing. Simple rule: if your cost isn't at least 3x below your selling price, you have a serious problem. Sooner or later you'll hit a ceiling and won't be able to grow.

Aim for businesses with margins above 60%, ideally 70–80%. Without margin there's no room to scale, and without scale it's a craft, not a business. Lithuania is full of businesses stuck at €20–30K in turnover: they can't afford marketing or better talent, so growth stalls.

## 5. Returning customers matter more than new ones

The other half of pricing is your returning-customer rate. The healthiest, fastest-growing businesses are built on subscriptions or frequent purchases: SaaS, B2B, high-repeat products. The reason is simple, your customer acquisition cost (CAC) has to be repaid by their lifetime value (LTV).

We built a telemedicine startup, Smart Vision. The model worked and we had funding, but acquiring a new customer cost €50–70, and customers only bought once or twice over a couple of years. When LTV equals CAC, growing and expanding into new markets becomes brutally hard, you need enormous investment to move at all.

When I ran a café chain in the UK, even in touristy London 60–70% of revenue came from loyal, regular customers, and only 30–40% from passers-by. That 60% covers your costs; the remaining 30–40% is your profit. This logic works in almost every business.

## 6. Know your funnel

"Funnel" sounds like jargon, but if you have a business and don't know your customer acquisition funnel, you either have a problem or you're leaving money on the table. A good example: a niche perfume brand we worked with. Selling perfume online is hard, you can't smell it. So we built a strong offer:

1. **A small first transaction.** Sample vials for a few euros, so the customer can try and get to know the brand. This eliminates the risk.
2. **Email marketing.** After the first purchase, an automated sequence kicks in.
3. **Targeted ads and content.** The ads and content that bring the customer back.

The result: every euro invested in marketing returns nearly eight. Over a two-month horizon we reach around €60K in turnover with no complicated tricks. The point: if you're an unknown brand, start with a small offer that removes the buyer's risk. Once people try it and love it, you can build on top.

## 7. Content and marketing win

Working alongside the founders of Burga, I understood something. Their product is tech accessories, but their real strength is elsewhere. First, they have an exceptionally strong marketing team with their own production studio. Second, they manufacture their own product. You won't see it from the outside, but that gives them a 20–30% margin edge that lets them grow aggressively. I have no doubt Burga is, or is about to become, a unicorn.

The takeaway is simple: if you don't have a big ad budget, you have to create content, both organic and paid. You need a strategy and a creative direction. Email marketing is still massively underused, and today it's mandatory.

## 8. The tools to start with

You don't need an expensive stack. Almost all of these have a free or cheap plan, and they're more than enough to build something good. The key is not to polish endlessly but to test: build a landing page, put €500 into Facebook ads, and you'll learn more than a month of perfecting the idea in your head.

- **Shopify** — an online store and fast idea testing.
- **Meta (Facebook/Instagram)** — advertising channel number one.
- **Google Ads + Workspace** — direct sales and email (~€5/mo).
- **Apollo** — a 300M+ contact database for cold outreach.
- **LinkedIn** — a growing, underused B2B channel.
- **Canva / Framer** — design and websites without a designer.
- **CRM (free plan)** — manage customer information.
- **Revolut** — business banking.
- **Notion** — tasks and processes.

And most importantly: don't obsess over the logo, colors, or brand at the start. Nobody cares yet. Black, white, and one color, and go. Obsess over one thing instead, your offer: what it is, what your funnel around it looks like, and how much it costs to acquire the first customer.

## How all of this became Callsy

With my brother I once built an e-commerce project that reached €300K in turnover within 12 months, organically and with Facebook ads, no outside investment. The breakthrough came when I started calling people who had visited the site but didn't buy, the abandoned carts.

About 70% of shoppers abandon their cart. When you call them, you learn an incredible amount about why people buy or don't. I'd sit down and make 10–20 calls a day. We adjusted the offer, the product, the pricing, and it exploded. That's exactly where Callsy was born: an AI agent that automatically calls abandoned carts, offers a personalized discount, and recovers lost revenue. A business is often born not from a genius idea, but from boring, repeated work.

> Nature's law is simple: what doesn't grow, dies. Business is the same. So never stop trying, repeating, and improving.
